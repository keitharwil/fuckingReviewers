Module 1: OVERVIEW OF DATA COMMUNICATION AND COMPUTER NETWORKING

Lesson 1: Data Communication

Data Communication – The exchange of data between two devices via a transmission medium. For data communication to occur, devices must be part of a communication system made up of hardware (physical components) and software.

History of Data Communication (Timeline)

Year Event
1940 Small Step for Man, Great Leap for Communications – George Stibitz sent computing commands over a teletype machine from Dartmouth College to his Complex Number Calculator in New York, marking a leap in networking technology.
1943 Teletype Computation – Stibitz’s work led to loop-based teleprinters and automated telegraphs. IBM adapted this to transmit punched cards at 25 bps.
1948 Teletype Modems to SAGE – Telephone systems using teletype communication modems transmitted multiple images to SAGE computers, increasing data transfer but reducing speed at the receiving end.
1958 AT&T Digital Subset – Boosted speed to 110 bps through AT&T computer modems called Digital Subsets, linking SAGE computers across the U.S. and Canada.
1962 Bell 103 Data Phone – First civilian commercial computer modem; allowed digital data transmission over standard telephone lines, though very expensive.
1962 Intergalactic Computer Network – J. C. R. Licklider (ARPA) proposed linking computers worldwide, enabling data and program sharing.
1965 Wide Area Network (WAN) – Thomas Marill and Lawrence Roberts created the first WAN, a precursor to ARPANET.
1977 Hayes 80-130A – First personal computer modem by Dennis Hayes and Dale Heatherington, allowed PC users to connect directly to phone lines.
1981 Hayes Smartmodem – Improved modem offering 300 bps, enabled auto-dialing, hanging up, and initializing. Later improved to 1200 bps.
Mid-1980s Fast Lane Modems – IBM PC clones led to Industry Standard Architecture (ISA) and PCI modem cards. This era marked the rise of Broadband Services.
1989 World Wide Web – Tim Berners-Lee proposed an information management system, becoming the foundation of today’s World Wide Web.
Mid-1990s Lower Costs, Faster Speeds – Broadband entered homes, PCI modems became standard in PCs/laptops, and global internet access expanded.
1996 The 56K Modem – Brent Townshend created the 56K modem with speeds of 56 kbps, doubling previous modems’ power. LANs became popular in businesses.
Early 2000s ISDN, ADSL, and Cable – Analog phased out in favor of ISDN (digital phone lines), ADSL (faster speeds over copper lines), and cable TV modems.
2002 Arrival of 3G – Third-generation (3G) wireless enabled mobile internet, video calling, and mobile TV. Required 200 kbps peak speeds.
Mid-2000s Wireless Age Matures – Broadband and wireless networks became mainstream; devices like early iPhones and tablets popularized wireless connectivity.
2009 4G/LTE – Introduced peak speeds of 100 Mbps (high mobility) and 1 Gbps (low mobility), supporting large file sizes and advanced services.

3 Fundamentals of data communication

  • DELIVERY. The system must deliver data to the correct destination.
  • ACCURACY. The system must deliver data accurately.
  • TIMELINESS. The system must deliver data in a timely manner.

Data Communication Concept

  • SENDER. The one who transmits message.
  • RECEIVER. The one who receives the message
  • MESSAGE. The data sent by the sender
  • MEDIUM. The material used where the message travels.
  • PROTOCOL. The set of rules that allow electronic devices to communicate with each other

Lesson 2. Data Representation

Data-refers to the symbols that represent people,events,things and ideas. Data can be a name, a number, the colors in a photograph, or the notes in musical operation. Data representation refers to the form in which data is stored, processed, and transmitted.

Data can be one of the following:

  • Text – Represented as a bit pattern, which is a sequence of bits.
    • Code – A set of bit patterns designed to represent text symbols.
    • Coding – The process of representing symbols using codes.
    • Unicode – A modern coding system that uses 32 bits to represent a symbol or character from any language in the world.
    • ASCII (American Standard Code for Information Interchange) – The first 127 characters of Unicode, also known as Basic Latin.
  • Numbers – Represented by bit patterns, directly converted to binary numbers to simplify mathematical operations.
  • Images – Represented by bit patterns and composed of a matrix of pixels, where each pixel is a small dot.
  • Audio – Refers to the recording or broadcasting of sound or music. Unlike text, numbers, or images, audio is continuous, not discrete.
  • Video – Refers to the recording or broadcasting of a picture or a movie. It can exist as a continuous entity or as a combination of images.

Module 2: NETWORK APPLICATION AND PHYSICAL STRUCTURES

Lesson 1: Network Application

Network applications are network software applications that utilize the Internet or other network hardware infrastructure to perform useful functions.

Network application can be defined as:

  • Pure Network Application
  • Stand-Alone Application

Pure Network Applications are created specifically for use in networks to transfer data and communicate. They have distinct user interfaces.

  • Email Programs – Allow users to type and send messages over a network; a fast and easy way of transferring mail from one computer to another.
  • File Transfer Protocol (FTP) – Facilitates file transfer between computers (client ↔ server).
  • Downloading – Obtaining files from a server to a workstation/client (e.g., downloading music or programs).
  • Uploading – Sending files from a workstation to a server (e.g., attaching documents or uploading photos).
  • Groupware – Applications that automate office functions like video conferencing and chatting. They help groups communicate, collaborate, coordinate, solve problems, negotiate, and increase productivity.

Stand-Alone Applications are applications that run on computers not connected to any network.

  • Notepad
  • Chrome
  • Calculator
  • Office

Network Criteria

A network must meet certain standards to function effectively. The three most important are performance, reliability, and security.

  • Performance – Measured by transit time and response time. It depends on factors such as the number of users, type of transmission medium, hardware capabilities, and software efficiency.
  • Reliability – Measured by frequency of failure, recovery time after a failure, and robustness during catastrophes.
  • Security – Involves protecting data from unauthorized access, preventing damage to data, and ensuring safe data development/transfer.

Lesson 2: Physical Structures (Network Topology)

Network Topology – Refers to the physical layout of a network. Two or more devices form a link, and two or more links form a topology. It is the geometric representation of the relationships between links and nodes (devices).

Types of Network Topologies

  • Point-to-Point
  • Bus Topology
  • Mesh Topology
  • Star Topology
  • Ring Topology
  • Tree Topology
  • Hybrid Topology

Point-to-Point (P2P) Topology

The simplest topology that directly connects two end systems with a common link. The full capacity of the link is dedicated to these two devices.

Advantages:

  • Very fast (only two nodes share the link).
  • Simple connectivity.
  • Easy to handle and maintain.

Disadvantages:

  • Only two nodes exist. If one fails, data transfer stops completely.

Bus Topology

A multipoint (multidrop) connection where more than two devices are strung together in a line or share a single link. Commonly used in LANs. The channel capacity is shared:

  • Spatially shared → multiple devices use the link simultaneously.
  • Timeshared → users take turns using the link.

Advantages:

  • Easy to connect a device to the network.
  • Cheaper compared to other network options.
  • No hubs or switches required.

Disadvantages:

  • Adding more devices slows down the network.
  • A break in the backbone can cause the entire network to collapse.

Ring Topology

A network setup where packets are sent in a circular path from computer to computer. Each computer checks if the packet is intended for it. Every device in the ring has a repeater that regenerates and forwards signals to keep data moving.

Advantages:

  • All data flows in one direction, reducing the chance of packet collisions.

Disadvantages:

  • Data must pass through each workstation, which can slow down the network.
  • If one workstation shuts down, the entire network is affected.
  • If the main cable breaks, the whole network fails.

Mesh Topology

A network where each node has multiple connections with other nodes. All nodes cooperate to distribute data.

Types of Mesh Topology:
  • Fully Connected Mesh – Every computer is connected to all other computers. The formula for the number of physical links is n(n-1)/2 (where n = number of nodes).
  • Partially Connected Mesh – Not all nodes are directly connected. Less costly compared to full mesh.

Advantages:

  • No traffic problem since each computer has a dedicated point-to-point link.
  • Very reliable: if one route is blocked, another can be used.

Disadvantages:

  • Installation is difficult because each node needs multiple connections.
  • More expensive compared to other topologies.

Star Topology

A network topology for LAN where all nodes are individually connected to a central device (hub or switch).

Advantages:

  • Easy to expand without disrupting the network.
  • Cable failure affects only a single user.
  • Centralized management of the network.

Disadvantages:

  • Heavy dependency on the central device.
  • Performance and number of nodes depend on the central device’s capacity.

Tree Topology

Also called Star-Bus Topology. Computers are connected like branches of a tree, combining features of bus and star topologies.

Layers:

  • Access Layer – Where computers are attached.
  • Distribution Layer – Mediates between the access and core layers.
  • Core Layer – The root of the network, a central point from which all nodes fork.

Advantages:

  • Damage to one node does not affect the other nodes.

Disadvantages:

  • Requires more cabling than star and bus topology.
  • More difficult to configure compared to other topologies.

Hybrid Topology

A type of network topology that combines two or more different topologies.

Advantages:

  • Combines the benefits of different topologies.
  • Very reliable.

Disadvantages:

  • Complex in design.
  • Expensive infrastructure.

Module 3: TYPES OF THE COMPUTER NETWORK AND THE INTERNET

Lesson 1: Classification of Computer Network

Computer networks are classified based on various factors, including Geographical Span, Interconnectivity, Administration, and Architecture.

Geographical Span

Geographically, a network can be seen in one of the following categories:

  • It may be spanned across your table, among Bluetooth enabled devices, ranging not more than a few meters.
  • It may be spanned across a whole building, including intermediate devices to connect all floors.
  • It may be spanned across a whole city.
  • It may be spanned across multiple cities or provinces.
  • It may be one network covering the whole world.

Interconnectivity

Components of a network can be connected to each other differently in some fashion.

  • Every single device can be connected to every other device on the network, making the network mesh.
  • All devices can be connected to a single medium but geographically disconnected, created a bus-like structure.
  • Each device is connected to its left and right peers only, creating a linear structure.
  • All devices connected together with a single device, creating a star-like structure.
  • All devices connected arbitrarily using all previous ways to connect each other, resulting in a hybrid structure.

Administration

From an administrator’s point of view, a network can be a private network which belongs to a single autonomous system and cannot be accessed outside its physical or logical domain. A network can be public, which is accessed by all.

Architecture

Computer networks can be discriminated into various types such as Client-Server, peer-to-peer or hybrid, depending upon its architecture.

  • There can be one or more systems acting as Server. Other being Client, requests the Server to serve requests. Server takes and processes requests on behalf of Clients.
  • Two systems can be connected Point-to-Point, or in back-to-back fashion. They both reside at the same level and are called peers.
  • There can be hybrid networks which involve network architecture of both the above types.

A network connects two or more computers to share resources (like printers), exchange files, and communicate electronically. They can be linked using various methods, including cables, radio waves, or infrared signals.

Types of Computer Networks

1. Personal Area Network (PAN)

A PAN is the smallest and most personal type of network. It connects devices within a very close range, typically up to 10 meters.

Examples:

  • Bluetooth-enabled devices like wireless headphones, keyboards, and mice connecting to a smartphone or computer.

2. Local Area Network (LAN)

A LAN connects hosts within a single, limited area such as an office, building, or campus. It can be wired, wireless, or a combination of both.

Key purpose:

  • Sharing resources like printers, file servers, and internet access among users.

Components:

  • Workstations: Computers used by a person to interact with the network, such as desktops, laptops, and tablets.
  • Servers: More powerful computers that provide services to the network and are often located in a secure area. They can operate without a display or keyboard.

3. Wireless Local Area Network (WLAN)

A WLAN is a wireless network that links two or more devices to form a LAN within a limited area. All devices that can connect to this network are called **stations**. Stations are divided into two main categories:

  • Wireless Access Points (WAPs): These are the base stations that transmit and receive radio frequencies. They act as a central hub for wireless communication.
  • Wireless Clients: These are the devices that connect to the network, such as laptops, smartphones, and printers equipped with a wireless network interface.

4. Metropolitan Area Network (MAN)

A MAN is a network that covers a large geographical area, typically an entire city. It's essentially a network formed by connecting multiple LANs. A MAN is larger than a LAN but smaller than a WAN.

5. Wide Area Network (WAN)

A WAN covers a very large geographical area, such as provinces or even a whole country. It's used to connect different LANs and MANs. Telecommunication networks are prime examples of WANs.

Types of WANs:

  • Point-to-Point WAN: This type of network connects only two communicating devices directly.
  • Switched WAN: This is a more complex network that connects more than two ends. It's the backbone of global communication, consisting of several point-to-point WANs linked by switches.

6. Internetwork

An internetwork (with a lowercase 'i') is created when two or more separate networks are connected to each other. This is the foundational concept for the global **Internet**.

The Internet and Its History

The Internet is the largest network on Earth, a "network of networks" that connects thousands of individual networks, including WANs, LANs, and home networks. It is a vast system with a hierarchical structure.

Internet Structure

  • Backbones: At the top level, these are large, high-speed networks owned by major telecommunication companies (e.g., AT&T, Verizon). They are also known as international ISPs.
  • Provider Networks: These smaller networks use the backbones' services for a fee and are connected to backbones or other provider networks. They are often called national or regional ISPs.
  • Customer Networks: These are the end-user networks at the edge of the Internet, such as those in homes and businesses, that use the services provided.

Internet History Timeline

1961

The concept of packet switching was introduced by Leonard Kleinrock. This method breaks data into small packets for transmission.

1967

ARPA presented the idea for ARPANET, a network that connected computers via specialized computers called IMPs.

1969

ARPANET became a reality, connecting the first four nodes at U.S. universities.

1977

An early internetwork successfully connected three different networks.

1981

The CSNET (Computer Science Network) was created to broaden network access.

1983

TCP/IP became the official protocol for ARPANET, which was then split into MILNET and a non-military ARPANET.

1986

NSFNET, a new backbone sponsored by the National Science Foundation, connected five supercomputer centers.

1992

The Web

Tim Berners-Lee invented the World Wide Web, which added commercial and public applications and fundamentally changed how we use the Internet.

Module 4: DATA FLOW, ACCESS NETWORKS AND PHYSICAL MEDIA

Data Flow

Data flow is the direction data travels between two devices. It's classified into three types:

  • Simplex: Data moves in only one direction. One device can only transmit, while the other can only receive. Think of a one-way street or a radio broadcast. The entire channel capacity is used for that single direction.
  • Half-Duplex: Both devices can transmit and receive, but not at the same time. A good analogy is a walkie-talkie, where one person speaks while the other listens. The channel's full capacity is used for one-directional data at any given moment.
  • Full-Duplex: Both devices can transmit and receive simultaneously. This is like a two-way street or a standard phone call where both people can talk at once. The channel's capacity is shared to allow for simultaneous two-way communication.

Accessing the Network

To access the Internet, users must physically connect to an Internet Service Provider (ISP). This connection is typically a point-to-point WAN.

1. Using Telephone Networks

  • Dial-Up Service: This is the original way to connect. Your computer uses a modem to "dial" a phone number to your ISP. While you're online, your phone line is busy, so you can't make or receive calls.
  • DSL (Digital Subscriber Line): This is a much better option. DSL lets you use your phone line for both voice calls and Internet access at the same time.

2. Using Cable Networks

  • Ethernet: This is what most of us use to connect devices in our homes and offices. An Ethernet cable is a networking cable that plugs into a device and provides a direct, reliable connection to a network.
  • Fiber Optics: This is the fastest type of physical connection. Fiber optic cables are made of tiny, hair-thin strands of glass that transmit data using pulses of light.

3. Using Unguided Mediums (Wireless)

Wireless networks use an unguided medium to send data. That means they transport electromagnetic waves through the air, without a physical conductor.

  • Wi-Fi: This is the most common wireless technology you use every day. Wi-Fi uses radio waves to provide high-speed Internet and network connections to devices like your phone and laptop. Your device connects to a **wireless access point (WAP)**, which is the hub of your wireless network.
How Unguided Signals Travel:
  • Ground Propagation: Radio waves travel close to the ground, hugging the Earth's surface.
  • Sky Propagation: Higher-frequency waves are sent up to the ionosphere and reflected back down to Earth, covering long distances.
  • Line-of-Sight Propagation: This is a direct, straight-line transmission from one antenna to another. This requires the antennas to be facing each other.

Physical Media

Physical media are the tangible materials used to transmit or store data in a network, such as copper and glass. There are three main types of wired transmission media.

1. Twisted Pair Copper Wire

This type of cabling consists of pairs of insulated copper wires twisted together. This twisting helps reduce electromagnetic interference. It is commonly used in telephone and Ethernet networks.

  • Unshielded Twisted Pair (UTP): This is the most common type. It lacks a metallic shield, making it the least expensive. UTP cables are classified into different categories based on their performance, with Category 6 being the fastest standard for UTP. It has a maximum speed of 1000 Mbps and a maximum length of 100 meters.
  • Shielded Twisted Pair (STP): This cable includes a metal foil or braided-mesh covering around the twisted pairs, which provides additional protection from interference. STP is moderately more expensive than UTP. It also has a maximum speed of 1000 Mbps and a maximum length of 100 meters.

2. Coaxial Cable

Coaxial cable, or coax, consists of a central copper conductor surrounded by an insulator, a metallic shield, and an outer jacket. The shield helps reduce outside interference. It carries signals at higher frequencies than twisted-pair cable.

  • Speed and Throughput: 10 to 100 Mbps
  • Average Cost: Inexpensive
  • Maximum Length: 500 meters
  • Common Uses: Cable television and internet providers.

3. Fiber Optics

Fiber-optic cable is made of fine strands of glass or plastic that transmit data as pulses of light. This medium offers the highest performance for data transmission over long distances.

  • Speed and Throughput: 10 Mbps to 100 Gbps (Gigabits per second)
  • Average Cost: Expensive
  • Maximum Length: 1000 meters
  • Common Uses: High-speed internet, cable television, and telephone networks.
