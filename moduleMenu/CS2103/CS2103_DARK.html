Module 1: OVERVIEW OF DATA COMMUNICATION AND COMPUTER NETWORKING

Lesson 1: Data Communication

Data Communication – The exchange of data between two devices via a transmission medium. For data communication to occur, devices must be part of a communication system made up of hardware (physical components) and software.

History of Data Communication (Timeline)

Year Event
1940 Small Step for Man, Great Leap for Communications – George Stibitz sent computing commands over a teletype machine from Dartmouth College to his Complex Number Calculator in New York, marking a leap in networking technology.
1943 Teletype Computation – Stibitz’s work led to loop-based teleprinters and automated telegraphs. IBM adapted this to transmit punched cards at 25 bps.
1948 Teletype Modems to SAGE – Telephone systems using teletype communication modems transmitted multiple images to SAGE computers, increasing data transfer but reducing speed at the receiving end.
1958 AT&T Digital Subset – Boosted speed to 110 bps through AT&T computer modems called Digital Subsets, linking SAGE computers across the U.S. and Canada.
1962 Bell 103 Data Phone – First civilian commercial computer modem; allowed digital data transmission over standard telephone lines, though very expensive.
1962 Intergalactic Computer Network – J. C. R. Licklider (ARPA) proposed linking computers worldwide, enabling data and program sharing.
1965 Wide Area Network (WAN) – Thomas Marill and Lawrence Roberts created the first WAN, a precursor to ARPANET.
1977 Hayes 80-130A – First personal computer modem by Dennis Hayes and Dale Heatherington, allowed PC users to connect directly to phone lines.
1981 Hayes Smartmodem – Improved modem offering 300 bps, enabled auto-dialing, hanging up, and initializing. Later improved to 1200 bps.
Mid-1980s Fast Lane Modems – IBM PC clones led to Industry Standard Architecture (ISA) and PCI modem cards. This era marked the rise of Broadband Services.
1989 World Wide Web – Tim Berners-Lee proposed an information management system, becoming the foundation of today’s World Wide Web.
Mid-1990s Lower Costs, Faster Speeds – Broadband entered homes, PCI modems became standard in PCs/laptops, and global internet access expanded.
1996 The 56K Modem – Brent Townshend created the 56K modem with speeds of 56 kbps, doubling previous modems’ power. LANs became popular in businesses.
Early 2000s ISDN, ADSL, and Cable – Analog phased out in favor of ISDN (digital phone lines), ADSL (faster speeds over copper lines), and cable TV modems.
2002 Arrival of 3G – Third-generation (3G) wireless enabled mobile internet, video calling, and mobile TV. Required 200 kbps peak speeds.
Mid-2000s Wireless Age Matures – Broadband and wireless networks became mainstream; devices like early iPhones and tablets popularized wireless connectivity.
2009 4G/LTE – Introduced peak speeds of 100 Mbps (high mobility) and 1 Gbps (low mobility), supporting large file sizes and advanced services.

3 Fundamentals of data communication

  • DELIVERY. The system must deliver data to the correct destination.
  • ACCURACY. The system must deliver data accurately.
  • TIMELINESS. The system must deliver data in a timely manner.

Data Communication Concept

  • SENDER. The one who transmits message.
  • RECEIVER. The one who receives the message
  • MESSAGE. The data sent by the sender
  • MEDIUM. The material used where the message travels.
  • PROTOCOL. The set of rules that allow electronic devices to communicate with each other

Lesson 2. Data Representation

Data-refers to the symbols that represent people,events,things and ideas. Data can be a name, a number, the colors in a photograph, or the notes in musical operation. Data representation refers to the form in which data is stored, processed, and transmitted.

Data can be one of the following:

  • Text – Represented as a bit pattern, which is a sequence of bits.
    • Code – A set of bit patterns designed to represent text symbols.
    • Coding – The process of representing symbols using codes.
    • Unicode – A modern coding system that uses 32 bits to represent a symbol or character from any language in the world.
    • ASCII (American Standard Code for Information Interchange) – The first 127 characters of Unicode, also known as Basic Latin.
  • Numbers – Represented by bit patterns, directly converted to binary numbers to simplify mathematical operations.
  • Images – Represented by bit patterns and composed of a matrix of pixels, where each pixel is a small dot.
  • Audio – Refers to the recording or broadcasting of sound or music. Unlike text, numbers, or images, audio is continuous, not discrete.
  • Video – Refers to the recording or broadcasting of a picture or a movie. It can exist as a continuous entity or as a combination of images.

Module 3: TYPES OF THE COMPUTER NETWORK AND THE INTERNET

A network connects two or more computers to share resources (like printers), exchange files, and communicate electronically. They can be linked using various methods, including cables, radio waves, or infrared signals.

Types of Computer Networks

1. Personal Area Network (PAN)

A PAN is the smallest and most personal type of network. It connects devices within a very close range, typically up to 10 meters.

Examples:

  • Bluetooth-enabled devices like wireless headphones, keyboards, and mice connecting to a smartphone or computer.

2. Local Area Network (LAN)

A LAN connects hosts within a single, limited area such as an office, building, or campus. It can be wired, wireless, or a combination of both.

Key purpose:

  • Sharing resources like printers, file servers, and internet access among users.

Components:

  • Workstations: Computers used by a person to interact with the network, such as desktops, laptops, and tablets.
  • Servers: More powerful computers that provide services to the network and are often located in a secure area. They can operate without a display or keyboard.

3. Wireless Local Area Network (WLAN)

A WLAN is a wireless network that links two or more devices to form a LAN within a limited area. All devices that can connect to this network are called **stations**. Stations are divided into two main categories:

  • Wireless Access Points (WAPs): These are the base stations that transmit and receive radio frequencies. They act as a central hub for wireless communication.
  • Wireless Clients: These are the devices that connect to the network, such as laptops, smartphones, and printers equipped with a wireless network interface.

4. Metropolitan Area Network (MAN)

A MAN is a network that covers a large geographical area, typically an entire city. It's essentially a network formed by connecting multiple LANs. A MAN is larger than a LAN but smaller than a WAN.

5. Wide Area Network (WAN)

A WAN covers a very large geographical area, such as provinces or even a whole country. It's used to connect different LANs and MANs. Telecommunication networks are prime examples of WANs.

Types of WANs:

  • Point-to-Point WAN: This type of network connects only two communicating devices directly.
  • Switched WAN: This is a more complex network that connects more than two ends. It's the backbone of global communication, consisting of several point-to-point WANs linked by switches.

6. Internetwork

An internetwork (with a lowercase 'i') is created when two or more separate networks are connected to each other. This is the foundational concept for the global **Internet**.

The Internet and Its History

The Internet is the largest network on Earth, a "network of networks" that connects thousands of individual networks, including WANs, LANs, and home networks. It is a vast system with a hierarchical structure.

Internet Structure

  • Backbones: At the top level, these are large, high-speed networks owned by major telecommunication companies (e.g., AT&T, Verizon). They are also known as international ISPs.
  • Provider Networks: These smaller networks use the backbones' services for a fee and are connected to backbones or other provider networks. They are often called national or regional ISPs.
  • Customer Networks: These are the end-user networks at the edge of the Internet, such as those in homes and businesses, that use the services provided.

Module 4: DATA FLOW, ACCESS NETWORKS AND PHYSICAL MEDIA

Data Flow

Data flow is the direction data travels between two devices. It's classified into three types:

  • Simplex: Data moves in only one direction. One device can only transmit, while the other can only receive. Think of a one-way street or a radio broadcast. The entire channel capacity is used for that single direction.
  • Half-Duplex: Both devices can transmit and receive, but not at the same time. A good analogy is a walkie-talkie, where one person speaks while the other listens. The channel's full capacity is used for one-directional data at any given moment.
  • Full-Duplex: Both devices can transmit and receive simultaneously. This is like a two-way street or a standard phone call where both people can talk at once. The channel's capacity is shared to allow for simultaneous two-way communication.

Accessing the Network

To access the Internet, users must physically connect to an Internet Service Provider (ISP). This connection is typically a point-to-point WAN.

1. Using Telephone Networks

  • Dial-Up Service: This is the original way to connect. Your computer uses a modem to "dial" a phone number to your ISP. While you're online, your phone line is busy, so you can't make or receive calls.
  • DSL (Digital Subscriber Line): This is a much better option. DSL lets you use your phone line for both voice calls and Internet access at the same time.
